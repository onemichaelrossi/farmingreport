How the figures, charts and turf advisor recommendations on the dashboard are worked out

← Back to dashboard

What this is

This dashboard is a predictive weather and turf-management aid for one or more UK sites, built from free public data. Each site has a postcode; a script running on a schedule pulls weather, soil and rainfall data, works out a handful of turf-relevant figures, generates plain-English recommendations from them, and publishes the results here as a static page — no accounts, server or build step needed to view it.

It is a decision aid, not a replacement for walking the ground. All figures are modelled from weather data rather than measured with on-site sensors, and the advisor's recommendations are simple rules applied to those figures — always confirm anything spray- or irrigation-related with a visual inspection first.

How the turf advisor works

The "Turf advisor" panel on the dashboard is a rules-based system, not a machine-learning model: every recommendation comes from comparing today's figures against a fixed, documented threshold, so the advice is explainable rather than a black box. Each recommendation carries a severity:

SeverityMeaning

WatchFigure is approaching a threshold — worth keeping an eye on over the next few days.

Growth-rate guidance (mowing frequency, whether to hold off feeding) comes from the trailing 7-day average Growing Degree Days per day: ≥ 10/day is treated as active growth, ≤ 4/day as slow growth. If nothing else is flagged, the advisor shows an "all clear" note rather than an empty panel.

Glossary

Growing Degree Days (GDD)
A running total of daily heat accumulation above a base temperature, used across agriculture and turf management to predict plant growth stages, mowing frequency and treatment timing more reliably than the calendar date alone. See the formula below.
Dollar spot
A common fungal turf disease (Clarireedia spp.) that produces small, straw-coloured, sunken lesions, particularly in cool-season grasses under warm, humid conditions with prolonged leaf wetness.
Smith-Kerns model
A published logistic regression model that estimates the daily probability of dollar spot outbreak from the 5-day trailing average relative humidity and air temperature. Widely used in turf management as a spray-timing decision aid.
ET₀ (reference evapotranspiration)
The modelled amount of water (in mm) that a well-watered reference surface would lose to evaporation and plant transpiration in a day. Used alongside rainfall to estimate whether a site is running a moisture deficit or surplus.
7-day water balance
Total rainfall minus total ET₀ over the trailing 7 days. A negative ("deficit") figure means more moisture has left than has fallen recently — a signal to consider irrigation. It is a simple running total, not a soil-moisture measurement in its own right.
Soil moisture (0–1cm)
Open-Meteo's modelled volumetric soil water content in the top 1cm, shown as a percentage. This is a model estimate, not a probe reading.
Leaf wetness probability
Open-Meteo's modelled daily-mean probability that leaf surfaces are wet (from dew, rain or humidity). Extended leaf wetness is one of the main drivers of fungal disease pressure.
Forecast vs actual
On the charts, solid lines are actual recorded/modelled days and the last dashed, dimmer segment is the forecast. Table views mark forecast rows with "(f/c)".
Analysis history
A compact daily snapshot (temperature, GDD, dollar spot risk, water balance, and that day's advisor severity) saved every time the fetch script runs, independent of whatever history window the weather APIs themselves return. This is what lets the trend charts keep growing over the lifetime of the site rather than being capped to a short rolling window. See below for details.
Flood/rainfall station
The nearest Environment Agency real-time rainfall gauge to the site (England & Wales only). Shown for situational awareness, not as a formal flood warning.

Techniques & calculations

Growing Degree Days are calculated per day as:

GDD(day) = max(0, ((T_max + T_min) / 2) − T_base)

where T_base defaults to 6°C (a common cool-season turf/grass baseline, configurable per site) and the daily values are summed from the season start date (default 1 January) to give the season-to-date total shown on the dashboard.

Dollar spot risk uses the published Smith-Kerns logistic regression model:

logit = −11.4041 + (0.0894 × RH5) + (0.1932 × T5)
risk % = elogit / (1 + elogit) × 100

where RH5 and T5 are the trailing 5-day mean relative humidity (%) and air temperature (°C). The model is only considered active when the 5-day mean temperature is between 10°C and 35°C; outside that range there's insufficient basis for a risk estimate and the dashboard shows "–" instead of a number.

Water balance is simply Σ precipitation − Σ ET₀ over the trailing 7 days.

All weather, soil-moisture, soil-temperature and leaf-wetness figures are Open-Meteo model outputs for the site's geocoded coordinates, not physical sensor readings at the site itself.

Analysis history in detail

Every time the fetch script runs, it writes a compact snapshot for that day — temperature, GDD, dollar spot risk, water balance, soil moisture and leaf wetness, plus that day's overall advisor severity — into data/sites/<site>-history.json. Each run upserts by date: re-running the script the same day updates that day's row rather than duplicating it, and a new day appends a new row. The very first run for a site backfills whatever history the weather API already returned, so the trend charts aren't a single dot from day one. The file is capped to the most recent 730 days to keep it a sane size after years of daily runs.

The dashboard's Growing Degree Days, dollar spot risk and temperature charts read from this file (falling back to the current run's own data if no history file exists yet), so those charts genuinely show "then vs now" as the schedule keeps running — not just whatever window the upstream weather API happens to return on a given day.
